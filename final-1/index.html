ANIMAL TESTING



Animal testing refers to any scientific experiments and procedures carried out on living animals for the purposes of basic biology and disease research, evaluating the efficacy of new medicinal products, and testing for the human health and safety of consumer and industrial products such as cosmetics, food additives, and industrial chemicals. Furthermore, before new medications are used by humans, researchers must test them to evaluate both their positive and negative effects. Animals are utilized because their biology is highly similar to that of humans, not only in basic activities like breathing, digesting, and mobility, but also in basic cell processes. Thus, they make excellent models.


Every year, more than 115 million animals are projected to be utilized in laboratory research around the world. However, the exact number is unknown because only a tiny percentage of countries collect and publish statistics on animal testing and research. The Animal Welfare Act, or AWA, is a federal law enacted in 1966 that regulates the treatment of animals in research facilities. It mandates that certain animals raised for commercial sale, used in research, transported commercially, or shown to the public receive minimal care and treatment. However, this rule exempts approximately 95% of animals that are tested on and gives only rudimentary protections for the remainder.

Why is animal testing changing?


On the surface, animals and humans share similar symptoms, but basic variations in genetics, physiology, and biochemistry can lead to divergent reactions to the illness and potential therapies. Such reasons and overreliance on animal models in several areas of illness study may have slowed rather than accelerated medical development.

Examples:

  • The FDA, officially known as the United States Food and Drug Administration, reported that 92% of drugs entering clinical trials after animal testing was rejected. Moreover, half of the 8% authorized were later withdrawn or relabeled as a result of severe or lethal side effects not found during animal testing.
  • According to a report by Dr. Jarrod Bailey, a geneticist and science advisor, 85 HIV/AIDS vaccines that have been successful in animal experiments have failed to protect humans, while some feel it rendered them more susceptible to the disease.
  • Published in the Journal “Annals of Internal Medicine,” it revealed that attempting to mimic human diseases intentionally producing symptoms in animals has scientific constraints that cannot be overcome. As a result, nine out of ten candidate treatments that appear to be safe and effective in animal research fail when tested in humans.


Some alternatives to animal testing


As the world evolved, new technology was introduced, and the need for animal testing became outdated and started to decrease in numbers as many countries began to prohibit the manufacture of such products. Nearly a hundred alternatives to animal experimentation have been discovered as of today. These were shown to be not only more efficient and cost-effective, but also more accurate. For instance...

Organs-on-chip: It is made up of human cells that have been cultivated in cutting-edge technology to imitate the structure and function of human organs and organ systems. According to the findings of a study released in 2014 by researchers in the department of engineering science, the chips can be used instead of animal tests and have been demonstrated to more accurately reproduce human physiology, illnesses, and treatment reactions. Watch this video to learn more about organs-on-chip.

Artificial Skin: EpiSkin™, EpiDerm™, and SkinEthic use artificial human skin to test how substances affect the skin. Perfectly reproduced skin models have the same properties and functions as normal human skin. As a result, the models in testing react to cosmetics even more genuinely than animal skin. Watch this video to learn more about artificial human skin.

Computer (in Silico) Modeling: Researchers have created a diverse set of sophisticated computer models that imitate human biology and disease progression. These models may accurately anticipate how new medications would behave in the human body and can thus replace the use of animals in exploratory research and many regular drug tests. b>Research With Human Volunteers: A method known as "microdosing" can provide crucial information on the safety of an investigational drug and how it is metabolized in people. Furthermore, sophisticated brain imaging and recording techniques involving human volunteers, such as functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), can be utilized to replace experiments in which rats, cats, and monkeys' brains are destroyed.
