How and why has it changed over the years?

On the surface, animals and humans share similar symptoms, but basic variations in genetics, physiology, and biochemistry can lead to divergent reactions to the illness and potential therapies. Such reasons and overreliance on animal models in several areas of illness study may have slowed rather than accelerated medical development.


As the world evolved, new technology was introduced, and the need for animal testing became outdated and started to decrease in numbers as many countries began to prohibit the manufacture of such products.

Some alternatives to animal testing


Organs-on-chip: It is made up of human cells that have been cultivated in cutting-edge technology to imitate the structure and function of human organs and organ systems. According to the findings of a study released in 2014 by researchers in the department of engineering science, the chips can be used instead of animal tests and have been demonstrated to more accurately reproduce human physiology, illnesses, and treatment reactions.


Artificial Skin: EpiSkin™, EpiDerm™, and SkinEthic use artificial human skin to test how substances affect the skin. Perfectly reproduced skin models have the same properties and functions as normal human skin. As a result, the models in testing react to cosmetics even more genuinely than animal skin. Watch this video to learn more about artificial human skin.


Computer (in Silico) Modeling: Researchers have created a diverse set of sophisticated computer models that imitate human biology and disease progression. These models may accurately anticipate how new medications would behave in the human body and can thus replace the use of animals in exploratory research and many regular drug tests.


Research With Human Volunteers: A method known as "microdosing" can provide crucial information on the safety of an investigational drug and how it is metabolized in people. Furthermore, sophisticated brain imaging and recording techniques involving human volunteers, such as functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), can be utilized to replace experiments in which rats, cats, and monkeys' brains are destroyed.
